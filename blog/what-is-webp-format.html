What is WebP? The Image Format Replacing JPG and PNG in 2026

📅 March 2026⏱️ 6 min read📂 Images👤 ConvertlyPro Team

WebP is a modern image format developed by Google that produces files 25–35% smaller than JPG — with equal or better visual quality. In 2026, all major browsers and platforms support it. Here's everything you need to know.

What is WebP?

WebP (pronounced "weppy") was introduced by Google in 2010, based on technology from the VP8 video codec. It was designed from the ground up to be the most efficient image format for the web — smaller than JPG for photographs, smaller than PNG for graphics with transparency.

Unlike JPG and PNG which were developed in the 1990s, WebP uses modern compression algorithms that understand how human vision perceives images. It prioritizes preserving detail that our eyes notice while aggressively compressing visual information we can't distinguish anyway.

Key Advantages of WebP

  • Smaller files: 25–35% smaller than JPG at equivalent quality. This directly improves page speed and Google PageSpeed scores.
  • Transparency support: Unlike JPG, WebP supports transparent backgrounds — combining the best of JPG (small size) and PNG (transparency).
  • Lossless mode: WebP can also be saved losslessly, like PNG — and is typically 26% smaller than PNG in lossless mode.
  • Animation support: WebP supports animations, like GIF, but with much smaller file sizes and better quality. Animated WebPs replace GIFs with far smaller files.
  • Both lossy and lossless in one format: You can choose lossy compression for photos and lossless for graphics — all in the same .webp format.

Browser and Platform Support in 2026

WebP is now universally supported. As of 2026, these browsers and platforms all support WebP natively:

  • ✅ Google Chrome (since 2010)
  • ✅ Firefox (since 2019)
  • ✅ Safari (since 2020 — macOS Big Sur, iOS 14)
  • ✅ Microsoft Edge (since 2018)
  • ✅ Android (native support)
  • ✅ WhatsApp (displays WebP images)
  • ✅ WordPress (supports since 5.8)

The only notable holdout is older Safari on macOS Mojave and earlier — but these represent less than 0.5% of users in 2026.

When Should You Convert to WebP?

Website images: Converting all website images to WebP is one of the highest-ROI optimizations available. A website with 20 images averaging 300 KB each (6 MB total) would reduce to ~4 MB with WebP — cutting load time by 30%.

E-commerce product images: Faster-loading product images directly increase conversion rates. A 1-second improvement in load time has been shown to increase conversions by 7% (Akamai research).

Mobile apps: Every KB saved in image assets reduces app download size and in-app loading time.

When to Keep JPG or PNG

Despite its advantages, WebP isn't always the right choice:

  • Sending to non-technical users: Recipients may not be able to open WebP in older software. If someone needs to print your photo in a shop, send JPG.
  • Social media direct uploads: Instagram and Facebook convert your uploads to their own formats. Upload JPG directly — the conversion to WebP happens on their servers.
  • Editing workflows: Don't use WebP as your working format in image editors. Keep PSD, TIFF, or PNG as master files and export to WebP only for distribution.
  • Email attachments: Email clients handle WebP inconsistently. Use JPG for photos in emails.

WebP for WordPress Websites

WordPress has supported WebP uploads since version 5.8. To serve WebP images on WordPress:

  1. Install a plugin like ShortPixel, Smush, or EWWW Image Optimizer
  2. Enable WebP conversion in plugin settings
  3. The plugin automatically serves WebP to compatible browsers and JPG/PNG to others

This "serve WebP with JPG/PNG fallback" approach is the industry best practice — WebP for modern browsers, original format for older ones.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Does converting JPG to WebP improve quality?
A: No — converting from JPG to WebP cannot recover quality already lost by JPG compression. Convert from original/raw files when possible. The advantage is smaller file size at the same quality level.

Q: Is WebP royalty-free?
A: Yes. WebP is open-source and royalty-free. Google released it under a BSD license. There are no licensing fees for using or serving WebP images.

Q: Can I open WebP files on my computer?
A: Yes, in 2026. Chrome, Edge and Firefox open WebP directly. On Windows, the Photos app handles WebP. On Mac, Preview opens WebP. For older software, you'll need to convert to JPG first.

When Should You NOT Use WebP?

WebP is excellent for the web, but there are situations where JPG or PNG remains a better choice. If you're sending images via email to people who may open them on older email clients like older versions of Outlook, WebP may not display correctly. Professional print workflows — for posters, brochures, or magazines — still prefer TIFF or high-quality JPG since print software rarely handles WebP. If you need maximum compatibility with legacy systems, software, or CDNs that don't support WebP, stick with JPG or PNG and convert to WebP only at the serving layer.

WebP and Google PageSpeed

Google's PageSpeed Insights specifically audits for "Serve images in next-gen formats" and flags sites that serve JPG or PNG instead of WebP. Switching your website images to WebP typically eliminates this warning and can add 10–25 points to your PageSpeed score — directly impacting your Google search ranking, since Core Web Vitals are a ranking factor. For an e-commerce site with 50+ product images, converting to WebP can reduce total page weight by 1–3 MB, cutting load time by 1–2 seconds on mobile.
