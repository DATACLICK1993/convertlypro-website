JPG vs PNG: Which Image Format Should You Use in 2026?

📅 March 2026⏱️ 7 min read📂 Image👤 ConvertlyPro Team

JPG and PNG are the two most common image formats on the internet — but choosing the wrong one costs you either quality or file size. This guide explains exactly when to use each.

The Core Difference: Lossy vs Lossless

The fundamental difference between JPG and PNG is how they compress image data.

JPG (JPEG) uses lossy compression — it permanently removes some image data to create smaller files. Each time you save a JPG, it recalculates which data to discard. The more you compress it, the more visual information is lost. This is why heavily compressed JPGs show "artifacts" — blocky patterns especially visible around edges and text.

PNG uses lossless compression — it reduces file size without losing any pixel data. A PNG can be saved, opened, edited, and re-saved thousands of times without any quality degradation. What you put in is exactly what you get out.

When to Use JPG

JPG excels for any image where slight quality loss is invisible to the human eye and file size matters.

  • Photographs — The compression artifacts are hidden within natural variation in photos. A JPG photo at quality 80–85 is indistinguishable from the original to most viewers.
  • Social media uploads — Facebook, Instagram, and Twitter recompress your images anyway. Uploading a large PNG doesn't preserve quality — the platform converts it. Upload an optimized JPG instead.
  • Website hero images and backgrounds — Smaller files load faster. A website background at 400 KB instead of 3 MB reduces page load time by 2+ seconds on mobile.
  • Email attachments of photos — A family photo album shared via email should always be JPG. PNG would exhaust attachment limits quickly.
  • Digital cameras — Nearly all cameras default to JPG for a reason: the storage savings allow thousands of photos per memory card.

When to Use PNG

PNG is essential in specific situations where quality cannot be compromised or transparency is required.

  • Logos and brand assets — Logos need to look perfect at any size on any background. PNG preserves every pixel exactly. Most importantly, PNG supports transparent backgrounds — critical for logos placed on colored website headers.
  • Screenshots and screen recordings — Text in screenshots needs to be crisp. JPG compression creates artifacts around letter edges that make text blurry. PNG keeps text perfectly sharp.
  • Digital illustrations and graphics — If you create art in Photoshop, Illustrator, or Figma with flat colors and sharp edges, PNG preserves these perfectly. JPG would blur the edges.
  • Images with transparent areas — This is the definitive use case. JPG cannot store transparency — any transparent area becomes white (or another fill color). PNG is the only choice for images that need to "cut out" onto backgrounds.
  • Source files and archiving — Always archive original files as PNG (or PSD/RAW). Never archive as JPG — each save adds more compression loss.

JPG Quality Settings Explained

When saving a JPG, most tools ask for a quality setting from 1–100. Here's what these numbers mean in practice:

  • 90–100: Near-lossless. Files are large (often bigger than PNG). Only use for master copies.
  • 75–85: Best balance. Quality looks perfect, files are 4–8× smaller than PNG. Recommended for web and sharing.
  • 60–74: Noticeable artifacts start appearing around edges. Acceptable for thumbnails.
  • Below 60: Heavily degraded. Only suitable for very small thumbnails where quality doesn't matter.

Quick Decision Guide

Use JPG when: ✅ It's a photograph ✅ File size matters ✅ No transparency needed ✅ Sharing on social media

Use PNG when: ✅ It's a logo or graphic ✅ Transparency is needed ✅ Text must be sharp ✅ You'll edit it again

What About WebP?

WebP is a newer format developed by Google that offers both lossy and lossless compression, plus transparency support. WebP files are typically 25–35% smaller than equivalent JPG files. Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Does converting JPG to PNG improve quality?
A: No. Converting a JPG to PNG does not recover lost quality — the compression damage from JPG is permanent. PNG simply stores the already-damaged pixels losslessly. Always start from the original source file.

Q: Which format does Google prefer for SEO?
A: Google doesn't have a stated preference between JPG and PNG. However, Google does prefer fast-loading pages — so using smaller JPGs for photographs will indirectly help your SEO by improving page speed.

Q: Can I use PNG for product photos on my e-commerce store?
A: For photos of products on white backgrounds, JPG is usually better — much smaller files with no visible quality difference. Reserve PNG for product images that need transparency (like clothing items shown without a background).
